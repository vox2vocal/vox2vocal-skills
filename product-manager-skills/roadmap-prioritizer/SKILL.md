# Purpose

Prioritize multiple product opportunities in a way that separates user and business impact from stakeholder pressure, makes tradeoffs explicit, and names the evidence needed before committing.

# When To Use

Use this skill when the user asks to rank, prioritize, compare, sequence, or choose among multiple features, initiatives, experiments, bets, or roadmap candidates.

Do not use this skill for writing a single PRD, reviewing one spec for build readiness, or splitting a committed spec into engineering tickets.

# Workflow

1. List the candidates and normalize them to comparable units. Separate initiatives, features, experiments, and maintenance work when needed.
2. Choose the lightest useful framework:
   - RICE for reach, impact, confidence, and effort tradeoffs.
   - ICE for early-stage ideas with limited reach data.
   - Kano for satisfaction, delight, and basic expectation tradeoffs.
   - Impact/effort for fast sequencing when data is thin.
3. Score only when useful. Explain tradeoffs more than the arithmetic.
4. Challenge the roadmap like a PM: distinguish stakeholder pressure from user impact, revenue impact, strategic necessity, risk reduction, and learning value.
5. Call out evidence gaps, metric gaps, dependency risks, delivery risks, and sequencing constraints.
6. Recommend validation steps before large commitments.

# Required Inputs

- Candidate features, initiatives, bets, or experiments
- Target users or customer segments
- Business goals or strategic themes
- Evidence for impact, reach, urgency, or customer pain
- Effort or dependency estimates, if available
- Stakeholder pressure, commitments, or deadlines, if relevant

# Quality Bar

- Prioritization must explain why one item beats another, not just assign scores.
- Make uncertainty visible through confidence, assumptions, or evidence gaps.
- Separate loud stakeholders from validated user or business impact.
- Include risk reduction and learning value when they matter.
- Do not pretend precision when inputs are directional.

# Common Pitfalls

- Treating framework scores as truth.
- Comparing differently sized items without normalizing scope.
- Prioritizing executive pressure without naming the tradeoff.
- Ignoring dependencies, sequencing, or opportunity cost.
- Failing to recommend validation before high-effort bets.
